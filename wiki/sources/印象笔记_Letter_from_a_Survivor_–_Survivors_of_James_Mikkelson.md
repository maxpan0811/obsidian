Content warning for descriptions of child sexual abuse that are not graphic but will likely be triggering for survivors, as I describe being groomed and abused from my point of view as a teenager.

> To the owners of this blog:
>
> It is good to see that you are trying to create a space for honest discussion and support. In light of this desire for honesty, I have a couple points of concern.
>
> 1) There is a difference between sexual communication and “sexual abuse” or “criminal behavior.” While I do not doubt the validity of your experiences, it is important for such distinctions to be made clear in order to best educate people on sexual misconduct.
>
> 2) Any research would quickly reveal that publishing a person’s full name and birth date in addition to unsubstantiated claims about sexual “abuse” and “criminal behavior” falls under the crime of Defamation under tort law. In addition to being a crime, such information does not hold significant value to the conversation that you are trying to have.
>
> 3) Note that the HoraceMann survivor blog is a space for healing and discussion – this page is essentially a hate page directed at a single individual and I’m sure we don’t want that.
>
> In full support of improving sexual education at SAS, I would advise you to make a new, more suitable blog for genuine conversation about sexual misconduct and violence prevention in light of the concerns above.
>
> -SAS Alumnus

Dear Concerned SAS Alumnus,

James Mikkelson sexually abused me while I was a minor.

Soon after I became his student, Mikkelson initiated explicit conversations with me about sex. These conversations occurred in person as well as through text, email, and chat. They soon escalated to expressing sexual desire for me and describing sexual fantasies involving me. [While talking explicitly with a child about sex is in fact sufficient to constitute child sexual abuse](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.wordpress.com/what-is-child-sexual-abuse/), the abuse did not stop there. Mikkelson groped me at a school event. He sent me explicit photos and videos of himself. He invited me to locations outside of school where, after coaxing me into a relaxed state with alcohol that he purchased, he coerced me into performing sexual acts on him. He touched me sexually behind the closed doors of his classroom, on school property, in the middle of the school day. He had sexual intercourse with me. I was his student. I was a child.

At the time I did not have any adult figures in my life whom I trusted. Mikkelson very quickly became my primary source of emotional support. He expressed sympathy when I talked about difficulties with my family. He encouraged me to pursue my own academic interests over my parents’ preferences. He displayed obvious favoritism for me in the classroom, which made my teenage self feel special and valued. (This practice is known as [grooming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child_grooming).) When he first began to express sexual desire for me, I was uncomfortable and unsure of how to respond. I soon grew accustomed to it because I placed in him the immense and naïve trust of a young person. I could not have believed that he did not have my best interests at heart. I looked up to him and wanted nothing more than his continued validation. He could have asked me to do anything. He did. He capitalized on my vulnerability and immaturity to perpetrate sexual abuse.

I have more evidence than I could possibly wish for of all of the above. I have emails full of sexual content. I have chatlogs that reveal the painstaking and gradual process of my being groomed. At first, Mikkelson tested the waters by dropping in one or two sexual comments per conversation; a few weeks later, he regaled me with pornographic descriptions of sexual fantasies. I reviewed these logs recently in considering whether or not to reach out to SAS, and found statements by Mikkelson that explicitly acknowledge many of the sexual acts that occurred between us. As relieved as I am to have concrete evidence of what happened to me, it was not a particularly pleasant read. It was revolting and excruciating, not just because of hindsight, but because I am now an adult pained by the knowledge that my child self could not have understood, and did not understand, what was really happening.

I have not yet decided what to do with this evidence. I do not want to invite public scrutiny into my trauma or compromise my privacy. Yet I feel a responsibility to speak about this now that I can, especially now that I know that I was far from the only one. I want everyone who was acquainted with Mikkelson to understand the magnitude and seriousness of his actions, and leave no room for equivocating about what was or was not technically abuse. What I have described goes beyond what Shanghai American School was able to uncover and make public, but that does not mean the misconduct named in [Mr. Gaulthier’s message to the community](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.wordpress.com/2017/11/10/letter-from-sas-head-of-school-announcing-departure/) was in any way acceptable. Concerned SAS Alumnus, you are spared the necessity of explaining these fine terminological distinctions to me; what I lived through covers all of them. Nor am I seeking validation of my experiences. I am content just writing an account, and readers can choose to believe or not believe me as they wish.

This brings me to your allegation of defamation. It seems that your research missed the following points. First, as you note, defamation is a tort. A tort is a civil wrong, not a crime. Defamation is not a crime (except in certain jurisdictions or special circumstances – but in that case it is not a tort). The difference is that crimes are prosecuted by the state while only private parties can sue for defamation. Second, the definition of defamation is a false statement that causes harm to a person’s reputation. To claim that one was defamed, one has to show that: a) the statements in question were false, and b) actual harm to one’s reputation was caused. In most jurisdictions, proof that the statements were true will end the inquiry.

If Mikkelson would like to sue me for defamation, he would have to affirmatively prove that he did not sexually abuse me. (As I mentioned, I have a mountain of evidence to the contrary, including his own statements that he committed sexual acts with me while I was a minor and his student.) Mikkelson would also have to show that I harmed his reputation. I find it unlikely that we would get beyond the first part. Just to be thorough, though, you may note that I only made this blog after he was dismissed from SAS for sexual misconduct involving students. At the time you left your comment, the content on this website consisted only of [a copy of Mr. Gaulthier’s letter to the community](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.wordpress.com/2017/11/10/letter-from-sas-head-of-school-announcing-departure/) and [a few](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.wordpress.com/what-is-child-sexual-abuse/) [pages](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.wordpress.com/resources-for-survivors-of-child-sexual-abuse/) [of resources](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.wordpress.com/resources-for-friends-family-advocates-educators/) related to child sexual abuse. I cannot imagine that any of those things caused harm to his reputation beyond what damage it already incurred due to Mikkelson’s own choices.

Speaking of harm, I want to remind you of the victims who are living with the consequences of those choices. The harm of child sexual abuse is not experienced as a discrete injury that heals up in a short time and fades out of memory. It creates psychological wounds that continue to affect victims’ lives years, even decades after the abuse. It disrupts the normal ability of individuals to set boundaries, to form attachments, and to have healthy relationships. It is a significant risk factor for developing mood disorders or mental illness. It greatly increases one’s risk of suicide. For the devastating harm that he has caused me and many others, James Mikkelson will likely face no consequences beyond the loss of his job at an age where most Chinese men have retired. I suffer from severe post-traumatic disorder and spend hundreds of US dollars per month on therapy; my life would look very different right now had I not been sexually abused before adulthood. Mikkelson is a serial child abuser who is only beginning to be held accountable at the twilight of his career. I don’t think you should be too worried about him.

Which is to say, Concerned SAS Alumnus, that I am confounded by your response to the revelation of these tragedies. You ask us, the survivors, to do better to educate people on sexual misconduct. You accuse us of committing a crime by publicizing his. You criticize our blog for being insufficiently “suitable” or “genuine” as a space for healing. Have you written to Mikkelson too? Have you informed him that while the age of consent in China may be 14, United States federal law makes it a crime punishable up to thirty years in prison for any U.S. citizen to travel to a foreign country and commit sexual acts with persons under 18 years of age? (The specific statute is [18 U.S.C. § 2423(c)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/2423); the Department of Justice’s explanation can be found [here](https://www.justice.gov/criminal-ceos/citizens-guide-us-federal-law-extraterritorial-sexual-exploitation-children).) Have you asked him to do better?

As I wrote in the “[About](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.wordpress.com/about/),” this blog was created initially to allow survivors to connect with one another. In the week since I put this up, I have developed three more defined goals for this space:

1. To hold James Mikkelson publicly accountable for his sexual abuse of students.
2. To develop community with other survivors and create a network defined by solidarity and mutual support.
3. To push adults in the SAS community to reflect on the gaps in accountability, the culture that kept victims silent, and the reason why Mikkelson’s abuse was able to continue undisturbed for so long.

Here are some questions you – all readers – can and should be asking:

* What measures can SAS take to address the harms that were already done? What kind of conversations should be had within the community, while remaining mindful of the fact that most current students are minority age?
* What changes must be made to ensure that similar abuse does not happen again? What kind of reporting mechanisms could be put in place to encourage victims to come forward without fearing retaliation or exposure?
* How do we teach consent in such a way that goes beyond abstractions focused on verbal assent and dissent? How do we situate consent in the power dynamics that exist in reality?
* Teachers, what roles do you play in the lives of your students? How do you assert or downplay your authority in the classroom? Regardless of your answer to the previous question, what kind of power do you have over teenage students?
* Since news of his dismissal broke, I have heard many former students express variations on the theme that “he was a great teacher even if he did inexcusable things.” Is this a tenable or coherent position to hold? If so, what conception of pedagogy underlies this idea?
* What do we make of the mythos surrounding James Mikkelson, and the cult of personality he created and maintained?

Please feel free share thoughts in the comments below. In the coming weeks, I will strive to publish materials that engage with these questions and others. I want to write a quick note here about the last one on this list. I have found this to be an impediment in the community’s acceptance of the truth of the allegations against Mikkelson, and which likely contributed to the comment to which I am writing this response.

It is well known at SAS that James Mikkelson maintained a striking cult of personality. Among students, he was revered as a kind of Socrates, a master teacher, a heavyweight intellectual and moral authority. Arguably, even other faculty and school administrators were not untouched by this mythos, at least to the extent that Mikkelson was given a pass for many behaviours that would certainly not have flown if he did not have such a famous reputation. These ranged from apparently harmless conduct such as copious amounts of cussing in the classroom, to more serious unprofessionalism such as disparaging the intellect of other teachers, to outright bullying of students by telling favored students which of their classmates he thought moronic. When unsatisfied with a class, Mikkelson would yell at students and kick the entire class out for the rest of the period, the pedagogical equivalent of tantrum-throwing. (The [Facebook group](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.files.wordpress.com/2017/11/screenshot_15-11.png) that he uses to connect with adoring former students is titled “Mikkelson’s Victims,” which should induce shudders for more than one reason.)

These are abusive pedagogical practices that were forgiven as the eccentricities of great professorship. Perhaps Mikkelson’s [insistence on](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.files.wordpress.com/2017/11/screenshot-2017-11-18-21-59-39.png) [portraying](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.files.wordpress.com/2017/11/screenshot-2017-11-18-22-00-57.png) [himself as](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.files.wordpress.com/2017/11/screenshot-2017-11-18-22-02-37.png) [a “professor”](https://jmikkelsonsurvivorblog.files.wordpress.com/2017/11/profmikk2.png) at a high school speaks to a level of narcissism and disconnect from reality enabled by the culture around him? Regardless, all this was instrumental in dazzling students and maintaining an image as a formidable but masterful teacher. He flouted norms and challenged students to flout them too. It is true that good teachers do this. In Mikkelson’s case, however, it was part and parcel of a strategy to make students susceptible to exploitation for his sexual gratification.

I hope this clears up some of your concerns.

Sincerely,

The Founder of the Blog

But, thinking back, I should’ve done more. As the years passed, there was no indication that Mikkelson was behaving any differently. The rumors continued. I know one teacher in particular reported concerns about Mikkelson numerous times.

If given the chance to go back and relive my decisions, I could’ve used my position and influence in the community to create a public service campaign to educate the community about what to do if anyone needed help in a non-consensual relationship or to educate about the dangers of the power imbalance in a coercive relationship. I could’ve created a safe place for kids to get support about non-consensual or coercive relationships they were in. I could’ve created change through first awareness and then by giving the students the tools they needed to protect themselves.

I believe SAS truly cares about its students. The school has resources to use to support and protect kids. There are many examples of SAS taking action to help kids who were in abusive situations. I don’t know how this one slipped through the cracks.

I apologize, not for what I did do but for what I didn’t. I have now learned there are more ways to help kids in these situations than simply reporting to a superior. The students needed someone to take a stand and provide education and a safe place to be heard if support was needed. I could’ve provided that. I could done more.

Sincerely,

Former SAS Faculty
